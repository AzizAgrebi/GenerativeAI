Amino acid sequences and MSAs

Aziz AGREBI

1. Introduction

Amino acids are the building blocks of proteins, essential molecules for life. Amino acid sequences determine the structure and function of proteins. Comparing these sequences across different species or within the same species enables us to understand the evolutionary and functional relationships between proteins. Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is a crucial method in this comparative analysis.

2. Amino acid sequences

An amino acid sequence is a linear chain of amino acid residues that makes up a protein. This sequence is usually represented by a series of letters, each of which corresponds to a particular amino acid (for example, A for Alanine, R for Arginine). For example, the sequence "MKTAYIAKQRQISFVKSHFSRQLEERLGLIEVQ..." represents the order of amino acids in a given protein.

These sequences are essential for protein function, as the protein's three-dimensional structure, which determines its function, is directly influenced by the amino acid sequence.

3. Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)

3.1 Definition

MSAs are alignments of several homologous amino acid sequences from different proteins or species. These alignments make it possible to compare sequences and identify conserved regions, i.e. portions of the sequence that have changed little over the course of evolution.

3.2 Why are they important?

MSAs are important for several reasons:

  • Identifying conserved regions: Regions that remain unchanged in many sequences may indicate parts of the protein that are essential for its structure or function.

  • Co-evolution: MSAs can be used to identify pairs of residues that evolve together, i.e. residues that undergo mutations in a coordinated fashion to maintain functional and structural interaction.

3.3 How to build MSAs?

Several algorithms and tools are available for creating MSAs. Here are some of the most common:

  • Clustal Omega: Uses a hierarchical clustering approach to align sequences. It is renowned for its speed and efficiency, even for large numbers of sequences (Thompson, Higgins, & Gibson, 1994).

  • MUSCLE (Multiple Sequence Comparison by Log-Expectation): Known for its accuracy and speed, it uses a step-by-step approach to build the alignment (Edgar, 2004).

  • T-Coffee: Offers high accuracy by combining several alignment methods to generate a consensus (Notredame, Higgins, & Heringa, 2000).

3.4 Applications

MSAs have many applications in molecular biology and bioinformatics, such as identifying conserved motifs important for protein function or structure, analyzing mutations to understand their impact on protein function, and identifying conserved regions in pathogen proteins for vaccine development.

The application we'll be focusing on next is structural prediction: information extracted from MSAs, such as coevolutionary contacts, is used by models like AlphaFold to improve the accuracy of structural predictions. By integrating this data, the model can make inferences about possible interactions between residues and thus better predict 3D structure. More on this in the following article.

4. 3D structure of proteins

4.1 Why is it useful?

The three-dimensional structure of proteins is crucial to their function. Proteins don't simply function as linear chains of amino acids; they fold into complex configurations that are essential for their biological activity. Knowledge of 3D structure helps us to understand how proteins interact with other molecules, how they catalyze chemical reactions, and how they contribute to cellular processes. 3D structures are determined by techniques such as X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and cryo-electron microscopy.

Understanding the 3D structure of proteins has several crucial applications:

  • Drug design: The 3D structure enables researchers to design drugs that bind to active sites on proteins, inhibiting or modifying their function in precise ways.

  • Protein engineering: By understanding the relationship between sequence and structure, scientists can modify proteins to improve their properties or confer new functions.

  • Study of diseases: Many diseases are caused by misfolded proteins or mutations that alter their structure. Knowledge of 3D structure enables us to understand these dysfunctions and develop targeted treatments.

  • Biotechnology: Proteins with specific 3D structures can be used in a variety of industrial processes, such as the manufacture of biofuels, the development of biobased materials, and the degradation of pollutants.

4.2 The rise of deep learning

However, experimental determination of 3D protein structure is often costly and time-consuming. Recently, machine learning techniques, in particular deep neural networks, have revolutionized the field of protein structure prediction. Models such as DeepMind's AlphaFold have shown impressive performance, capable of predicting the 3D structure of proteins with an accuracy approaching that of experimental methods.

These models represent a major advance in computational biology. It offers promising prospects for scientific research, medicine and biotechnology, transforming our ability to understand and manipulate the fundamental biomolecules of life.
